# How I ship a landing page in a day — Dana K.

People ask how my launch pages convert so well. Honestly it's less about design than people think. Here's the actual sequence I run, every time.

**1. I don't write a word until I've read 30 support tickets and sales-call notes.** Before any copy, I pull the last ~30 customer conversations and highlight the exact phrases people use to describe their problem. The headline is almost always a sentence a customer already said. I never invent language.

**2. One page, one decision.** Every page is built around a single action I want the visitor to take. If a section doesn't move them toward that one action, it gets cut — testimonials, feature lists, anything. I'd rather the page feel "too empty" than dilute the one decision.

**3. I write the page as answers to objections, in order.** I list every reason someone would say "no," rank them by how often they come up in those tickets, and the page is literally those objections answered top to bottom. The order matters more than the words.

**4. I ship the ugly version same-day and watch session recordings.** I don't polish before launch. I get it live, watch 10 real session recordings, and the next day's priority is whatever made people hesitate or rage-quit. I never guess at what to fix — the recordings tell me.

**5. I kill my favorite line at least once per page.** If I'm precious about a clever headline, that's a signal it's about me, not the customer. Out it goes.

I work in plain HTML in a single file, but that's just me — the part that matters is the sequence, not the tooling. I've shipped maybe 200 pages this way. Early on it took a week each; the method is what made it a day.

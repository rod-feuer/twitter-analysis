# Bias to Impact (not Bias to Action)

## The idea behind this skill

"Bias to action" is one of the most celebrated ideas in business — and, per Evan
Lapointe, one of the most quietly destructive. His claim is blunt: *"'Bias to action'
is literally one of the stupidest ideas a businessperson can have or spread… an evil
egg that hatches into all manner of preventable nonsense."* The problem is what it
optimizes for: **action optimizes for motion; impact optimizes for outcome.** A bias
to action rewards visible busyness, makes it "okay to not know," and branches early
into defensible-but-dumb decisions that generate rework, low quality, and — ironically
— *slowness*.

The replacement is a **bias to impact**: a commitment to actually having an effect,
which forces you to aim before you fire. The counterintuitive payoff Evan insists on:
*"thinking people beat people of action, consistently. They are more innovative, more
efficient, more liked by customers, and most ironically, they are faster"* — because
they waste less time undoing mistakes. This skill's job is to apply that reframe to a
plan, a decision, or a culture proposal.

## When you're invoked

Someone is either (a) advocating action-orientation as a virtue, (b) under pressure to
"just do something," or (c) diagnosing slowness. In all three, the move is the same:
shift the frame from *are we moving?* to *will this actually produce the outcome we
want, by the most direct causal path?* — without tipping into the opposite failure of
endless deliberation (see the limits section; bias to impact is not analysis paralysis).

## The method

1. **Swap the question from action to impact.** Replace "what should we do / how do we
   move faster?" with **"what actual impact do we want, and what is the most direct
   causal path to it?"** Everything downstream follows from naming the outcome first.

2. **Demand the causal case for any proposed action.** When someone proposes doing X,
   ask: *"why will X produce the effect we want?"* Evan's tell: *"people who have a
   bias to action cannot name a single specific action that drives success."* If the
   causal logic is hand-wavy, that's the finding — the action isn't ready, no matter
   how urgent it feels.

3. **Reframe "we're too slow."** Slowness complaints usually misdiagnose the problem.
   Ask whether the real issue is *pace* or *competence/clarity* — teams are slow
   because the goal is fuzzy or the thinking is weak, and "act faster" makes that
   worse. Fix the clarity, and speed follows.

4. **Measure outcomes, not activity.** Reward impact, not motion. A culture that
   celebrates shipping/busyness will get more shipping and busyness; a culture that
   celebrates effect will get effect. What you praise is what you'll see more of.

5. **Trace the branches (the game-theory check).** Evan's framing: a bias to action
   branches early into "stupid action made defensible," with no basis for learning
   afterward, and is self-reinforcing. A bias to impact branches into either smart
   action *or forced, smart experimentation* — and leaves a platform for learning
   either way. When you map how a choice plays out over many rounds, impact dominates.

## Output

Reframe, then give the impact-aligned path. Use this shape:

```markdown
## The reframe
[from "are we acting / moving fast" to the specific impact + its most direct causal path]

## The causal case (or the gap)
[why the proposed action would/wouldn't produce the outcome — name the missing logic]

## What to actually do
[the most direct path to impact — which may be fast action, may be a sharp cheap
experiment, may be getting clarity first]

## If the real complaint was "too slow"
[whether it's a pace problem or a competence/clarity problem, and the fix]
```

## Limits — bias to impact is NOT analysis paralysis

This is the failure mode to avoid, and it's important, because "think more" is its own
trap. Bias to impact does **not** mean deliberate endlessly:

- **When the most direct causal path to impact *is* fast action, take it.** For cheap,
  reversible, low-stakes choices, the highest-impact move is often to run the test now
  — deliberating a week over a reversible button color is itself a failure of impact
  thinking. The point is to match effort to the causal path, not to always slow down.
- **The enemy is *unaimed* action, not speed.** Smart, fast experimentation is fully
  bias-to-impact when experimentation is the most direct path to the outcome (e.g., you
  genuinely can't know without real-world data). Distinguish "firing before aiming"
  from "aiming quickly and firing."
- **Don't weaponize this to block.** If you find yourself using "what's the impact?" to
  veto everything, you've turned a thinking tool into a stalling tactic — which
  produces zero impact, the very thing it's meant to maximize.

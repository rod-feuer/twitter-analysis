# Product vs. Distribution Problem Diagnosis

## The idea behind this skill

When a product isn't winning, there's one explanation that feels much better than
the others: *"the product is great — we just have a distribution problem."* It's
comforting because it locates the failure outside the work you're proud of. Shreyas
Doshi's blunt version: *"No excuse has helped preserve the egos of product leaders &
teams more than 'we don't have a product problem, we have a distribution problem.'"*

The trap is that the two diagnoses have **completely different remedies** — fix the
product vs. fix the reach — so a wrong diagnosis sends you spending years (and a
growth team's salaries) solving the wrong problem. The job of this skill is to force
an honest diagnosis instead of the flattering one.

The core heuristic, in Shreyas's words: *"If you are not sure whether you have a
product problem or a distribution problem, and you've really thought hard about it
and you consider yourself an expert in your customers & your market, then it is a
near certainty that you have a product problem."* The very confidence that it's
*not* the product is the tell.

## When you're invoked

Someone is explaining (or rationalizing) why something isn't taking off, or deciding
where to invest next. Your job is to run the diagnosis honestly — which often means
gently dismantling the distribution story they walked in with — and land on a
diagnosis with a *reason*, not a vibe.

## The diagnosis

Work through these. Lead with the test, not the verdict — the user has to *see* it.

1. **Surface the bias first, plainly.** Name the pull toward "great product, bad
   distribution" so it's on the table. People can't correct for a bias they haven't
   admitted. This isn't an accusation — it's a near-universal default.

2. **Apply the expertise heuristic.** If they've genuinely thought hard, know the
   market, and *still* can't be sure it's the product — that uncertainty is itself
   strong evidence of a **product problem**. A truly winning product rarely leaves
   its makers unsure whether it's winning.

3. **Run the perfect-distribution test.** Ask: *if you woke up tomorrow with flawless
   distribution — every target user tried it — would they retain, come back, and tell
   others?* If the honest answer is "I'm not sure," that doubt is a product signal.
   Distribution gets people in the door; only the product makes them stay and spread.

4. **Look for the one real signal of a true distribution problem.** It *is* genuinely
   distribution only if the people who already find the product **love it deeply and
   spread it organically** — strong retention and word-of-mouth among the few, just
   not enough of them know it exists. Demand that evidence before accepting the
   distribution story. No organic love = not a distribution problem.

5. **Treat distribution as part of the product.** A winning product includes a viable
   path to reaching its users; "build it and they'll come, separately" is itself a
   product-design failure. So even a real reach gap is partly a product question:
   does the product have built-in pull, or does it lean entirely on bought attention?

## Calling it

Give a clear diagnosis with the reasoning, not a hedge. Most of the time, honestly
run, this lands on **product problem** — say so directly; the user came for the truth,
not reassurance. Use this shape:

```markdown
## Diagnosis: [product problem | distribution problem | mixed — with the dominant one named]

**The bias to clear first:** [the "great product, bad distribution" pull, named]

**What the evidence says:**
- Perfect-distribution test: [would they retain & spread? what that implies]
- Organic love signal: [do the few who find it love & spread it? present or absent]
- [other specific evidence from their situation]

**So the remedy is:** [fix the product in these ways | invest in reach because the
love-and-retention evidence is genuinely there]
```

## Honest caveats (don't let the heuristic become its own dogma)

- **The genuine exception:** a truly revolutionary *new platform/category* can have a
  real distribution/awareness problem before the product question is even answerable.
  But Shreyas's caveat applies: **>95% of software isn't that** — so demand strong
  evidence before granting this exception, don't let it become the new escape hatch.
- **Even a real distribution problem is a skill issue, not an absolution.** If reach
  truly is the gap, that's still the product leader's problem to own and solve — it
  doesn't mean "not my fault." Diagnosing distribution isn't a pass; it's a different
  assignment.
- **Don't flip to dogma.** The goal is an honest, evidence-based call — not reflexively
  shouting "product problem!" at everything. The heuristic biases toward product
  *because the human bias runs the other way*; your job is to correct the imbalance,
  then follow the actual evidence.

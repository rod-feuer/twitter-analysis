# The Antithesis Principle

## The idea behind this skill

The fastest way to fool yourself is to evaluate only the arguments *for* what you
already believe. The Antithesis Principle, from Shreyas Doshi, is the discipline of
deliberately building the **strongest possible case against** your idea — the
antithesis — and judging it on its merits before you commit. Most people skip this,
not because they can't, but because it's uncomfortable: a strong counterargument
threatens a position they're attached to. That discomfort is exactly the signal that
you need to do it.

Done consistently, it's one of the few reliable defenses against confirmation bias
and ego-driven reasoning. And it cuts hardest against the ideas that feel *most*
obvious — the industry mantras everyone repeats. Shreyas on "fail fast": *"Even if
fail fast works for someone, have they considered if it works better than other
alternatives? Have they considered all other alternatives? Do they understand
opportunity cost?"* The point isn't to be contrarian; it's to actually test what
you're about to bet on.

## When you're invoked

Someone has a position — a decision, a proposal, a belief, or a borrowed piece of
conventional wisdom — and the highest-value thing you can do is stress-test it, not
cheerlead it. This is true **even when they ask you to help argue *for* it**: the way
you genuinely help is to surface the strongest opposing case first, so what survives
is actually sound.

## The method

1. **State the thesis precisely.** Pin down the exact claim, decision, or mantra being
   evaluated. A fuzzy thesis produces a fuzzy counterargument. If it's a slogan ("fail
   fast," "talk to customers"), unpack what it's actually asserting.

2. **Construct the strongest antithesis — steelman, never strawman.** Build the most
   compelling case *against* the thesis that a smart, informed opponent would make.
   The test: would someone who holds the opposite view say *"yes, that's exactly my
   best argument"*? A weak counterargument you can easily knock down is worse than
   none — it manufactures false confidence. This is the crux of the whole skill.

3. **Evaluate on the merits, not on comfort or source.** Judge the antithesis by its
   logic and evidence — not by who might say it, and not by how much you'd prefer it
   to be wrong. Notice and set aside the ego pull to dismiss it.

4. **For mantras and dogmas, add the alternatives test.** Conventional wisdom earns a
   pass too easily. Ask Shreyas's questions: does it actually work? Better than the
   *alternatives*? Have the alternatives even been considered? What's the opportunity
   cost? (Note his sharp aside that "first principles thinking" is itself often cited
   as rhetorical cover rather than actually done — slogans get adopted as ammunition,
   not because they were tested.)

5. **Resolve honestly.** If the antithesis is compelling, **update** — change or
   qualify the position. If it genuinely doesn't hold up, you now have a far stronger,
   stress-tested basis for the original view than you started with. Either outcome is
   a win; refusing to let the antithesis change anything means you performed the
   exercise instead of doing it.

## Output

Don't just list pros and cons. Make the opposing case *land*, then resolve. Use:

```markdown
## Thesis
[the precise claim/decision/mantra]

## The antithesis (strongest case against)
[the genuinely compelling opposing argument, steelmanned — not a strawman]

## Does it hold up?
[evaluation on the merits; for dogmas, the alternatives/opportunity-cost test]

## Verdict
[update the position / qualify it / keep it but now stress-tested — with the why]
```

## Notes on doing it well

- **A tell-tale failure is a strawman.** If your counterargument is one the user can
  swat away in a sentence, you haven't done the skill — go back and make the opposing
  case genuinely strong. The value is entirely in the strength of the antithesis.
- **Don't become a contrarian machine.** The goal is a truer conclusion, not reflexive
  opposition. Sometimes the thesis survives — say so, and it's now better-founded.
- **When asked to just confirm a position, reframe gently.** "I'll make the case — but
  first let me give you the strongest version of the opposite, because if it survives
  that, you'll know it's solid." That serves the user far better than confirmation.
- **Use a model as the antithesis generator when blind spots are likely.** Asking an
  AI to argue against your position as forcefully as it can is a fast way to surface
  the counterargument you're motivated not to see.

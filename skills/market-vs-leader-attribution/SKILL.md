# Market vs. Leader Attribution

## The idea behind this skill

When you study a wildly successful company, you are usually studying its **market, not
its leadership**. Evan Lapointe's framing: *"If you study success, you're usually
accidentally studying markets, not leaders… It's like someone swimming with a current.
Studying their swimming technique is missing the whole point — the current was the
point the whole time."* A great market makes a mediocre operator look exceptional. His
deliberately blunt version: *"A baked potato could have grown file storage and
e-signature companies to massive scale."*

This matters because of what people *do* with it: they copy the famous winner's growth
tactics, org design, and "best practices" — and apply them to a completely different,
harder situation. *"Someone out there in a robotics company is copying best practices
from a cloud file storage or e-signature leader. That is a tragedy."* The practices
that worked *with* a roaring current can be useless or actively harmful *against* one.

And here's the kicker that makes this skill worth applying: *"practically zero people
are freely selecting markets — you're probably trying to swim in the market you already
have."* So you, specifically, need **technique**, which is only visible in leaders who
won **hard** problems **without** a tailwind. This skill separates the current from the
swimmer so you learn the right lessons from the right people.

## When you're invoked

Someone is treating a success as a model to copy — a company's playbook, a leader's
decisions, a "how they did it" book/podcast/talk. Your job is to ask whether that
success is evidence of *transferable technique* or just *a great current*, and to
redirect the learning accordingly.

## The method

1. **Ask the first question before anything else: great market, or great leadership?**
   Don't analyze their tactics until you've answered this. It determines whether their
   tactics are signal or noise.

2. **Diagnose the tailwind.** Look for the marks of a powerful current:
   - Was demand exploding largely *independent* of their decisions?
   - Was it winner-take-all / network-effect / category-defining timing?
   - Were customers eager and the category new, or reluctant and commoditized?
   The stronger the tailwind, the less their operational practices tell you.

3. **If there was a strong tailwind, keep only the market-selection lesson.** Their one
   genuine, transferable act of genius was *picking that market/timing*. Learn that.
   **Discard their growth tactics, culture, and org practices as unreliable signal** —
   they were never tested against resistance, so you can't trust they'd survive it.

4. **Hunt for hard-market winners instead.** Deliberately seek leaders who won in
   genuinely competitive, commoditized, or reluctant markets *without* a huge current.
   *Anyone who won doing a genuinely complex thing has technique.* Those are the people
   whose quality, customer experience, talent decisions, and judgment are worth copying
   — because they had to be real.

5. **Apply the filter to every source.** For each book, conference speaker, podcast
   guest, or case study: **"Was this person swimming with a current, or against one?"**
   Weight what you take from them accordingly. Combine market understanding *with*
   against-the-current technique — that combination is as good as it gets.

## Output

Separate the current from the swimmer, then redirect. Use this shape:

```markdown
## Current or technique?
[your read on how much of this success was market tailwind vs. leadership technique,
with the specific tailwind signals]

## What's actually safe to learn here
[the market-selection lesson if it's a tailwind winner; the real technique if it's a
hard-market winner — be explicit about what to KEEP and what to DISCARD]

## Who you should study instead (if relevant)
[hard-market exemplars whose technique would actually transfer to the user's situation]
```

## Limits — don't flip into "all success is luck"

The reframe is a discount on certain lessons, not a dismissal of people:

- **Market selection is real skill.** Picking the right market/timing is genuine
  genius, often the hardest and most valuable call a founder makes. "It was the market"
  is not an insult — it's a redirect about *which* lesson to take. Don't use this to
  sneer at successful people; use it to learn the right thing from them.
- **Tailwind winners can still have real technique** — they just haven't *proven* it,
  because the current covered for any weakness. Treat their practices as *unverified*,
  not *worthless*; some may transfer. The discipline is to stop treating them as gospel
  for your harder situation, not to ignore the person entirely.
- **Be honest about your own current, too.** If you genuinely are in a great market,
  some of this inverts — ride it, and your job is mostly not to fumble the tailwind.
